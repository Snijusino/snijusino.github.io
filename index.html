The Root File: A Story of Silence of Me

My life started like anyone else’s, but the foundation was cracked early. I remember being in the first grade. It was midnight. My parents were fighting over nothing, and suddenly, the anger turned on me. I was beaten so hard that I tried to scream, but nothing came out. My throat felt blocked—a physical barrier that stopped my words. All I could do was cry.

That was my first trauma. It became a root file in my system—a file that can never be deleted. Every time someone bullied me, every time my family or friends turned on me, that file would execute. My mouth would shut. Even when I had the truth on my side, I couldn't speak. When I finally tried to tell my family how I felt, they called it "drama." They called me "weak." So, I stopped trying to be heard. I started being a fake. I created a personality to show the world, while the real me stayed unknown—drifting like a ghost in a location that no longer exists.

The Black Screen and the First Script

By 8th grade, I was already drifting. I was smoking, using tobacco pouches, anything to get a hit of cheap dopamine because the world felt so gray. The society had already labeled me a "bad kid," so I just accepted the name.

Then the pandemic hit in 2019. I got my first device for online classes. While others saw a tool for school, I saw an escape. I opened a terminal for the first time—a black screen with a blinking cursor. It didn't judge me. It didn't call me weak. I taught myself to code. I wrote a script, shared it with a community, and for the first time in my life, I felt like I was something.

Ashna: The Only High

I remember the day I went back to offline classes. I looked up and saw her: Ashna. I was locked in her eyes. When she asked my name, I was so overwhelmed I literally ran away. I came back the next day just to say my name: ~Сниджу.Ашна.

We became best friends. I loved her, but I was terrified that telling her would delete the only good thing I had. When I finally proposed, I didn't get a "yes" or "no"—I got a beating. I was told she was the one who told her family to beat me. I believed the lie. I stayed away from her for months, falling deeper into smoking to forget the pain.
